## The Glivenko-Cantelli theorem
Before tackling the Glivenko-Cantelli theorem we must define some notions.

**The cumulative distribution function**
Let $$X$$ be a random variable. The *CDF* of $$X$$ is the function given by the following:
<center>$$F_X(x)=P(X\leq x)$$</center>
Intuitively this function gives us for a given *x* the probability that the random variable *X* will take a value less then or equal *x*.

**The empirical distribution**
Let $$X_1,...,X_n$$ be a colection of independent and identical distributed random variables with *cumulative distribution function* $$F_X$$. Then the *empirical distribution function* will be denoted as $$F_n(x)$$ and defined for $$x\in\mathbb{R}$$ by
<center>$$F_n(x)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} I_{[X, \infty)}(x)$$</center>
where $$I_A(\omega)$$ is the indicator function for set *A*.

**The Glivenko-Cantelli theorem**
Let $$X_1,...,X_n$$ be a colection of independent and identical distributed random variables with *cumulative distribution function* $$F_X$$, and let $$F_n(x)$$ denote the *empirical distribution function*. Then, as n tends to $$\infty$$,
<center>$$P[sup_{x\in\mathbb{R}}|F_n(x) - F_X(x)|\rightarrow 0]=1$$</center>
Intuitively this is telling us that for n large, the empirical distribution approximate the cumulative distribution.
