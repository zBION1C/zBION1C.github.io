## Ito calculus
The **Ito calculus** extends the methods of calculus to stochastic processes. Let's explore this new branch of mathematics using a simple example.

Consider a coin-tossing experiment
- Head: you win 1$
- Tail: you give me 1$

Let $$R_i$$ be the outcome of the *i*-th toss
- $$R_i=+1$$ with probability $$\frac{1}{2}$$
- $$R_i=-1$$ with probability $$\frac{1}{2}$$

It's easy to convice ourself that $$R_i$$ is a **random variable**.

Now let 

<center>$$S_i=\sum_{j=1}^{i}R_j$$</center>

be the total amount of money you have up to and including the *i*-th toss.
What we defined above is a **stochastic process**, more preciseley a **random walk**.
As the number of tosses approaches $$\infty$$ then the random walk becomes the **brownian motion**
stochastic process, as seen with the *Donsker theorem*. The *Brownian motion* can be define with the following *stochastic differential equation*:

<center>$$dS_t=\mu S_tdt+\sigma S_t dW_t$$</center>

In general a stochastic process $$Y_t$$ can be defined as follows:
<center>$$Y_t = \int_{0}^{t}HdX$$</center>
where *H* is an integrable stochastic process.
The integral definition above is sometimes written in differential form (like the example above)
<center>$$dY = H dX$$</center>
which is equivalent to
<center>$$Y-Y_0=H X$$</center>

Ito calculus provide a method to find the solutions of this particular type of differential equations. Even though with Ito calculus we can find analitically a solution for this type of differential equations, it is very **hard** to do so for particulary intricate stochastic processes. For this reason Ito calculus is used in conjunction with computational simulation methods of stochastic processes to study them.
